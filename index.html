Corporate Landlords Are Reshaping Boston — Unevenly

An exploratory analysis of corporate ownership, owner occupancy, and their diverging trajectories across Boston's 21 neighborhoods, 2004–2024.
Disha  |  Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions  |  Dataset: Corporate Ownership & Occupancy Over Time
§ 01

Overall Questions

Before touching the data, I grounded my investigation in two background readings: the Homes for All MA report on COVID-era evictions in Massachusetts, and the New York Times deep dive into 83 million eviction records. Both surfaced the same core concern: that the identity of a landlord — corporate vs. resident — shapes how tenants are treated, especially in crises. With that framing, I posed three questions targeting substantially different angles of the dataset.

Question 1

Is there a structural tradeoff between corporate ownership and owner occupancy — and how strong is it across Boston's neighborhoods?

Motivation The Homes for All MA report argues that "live-in or live-nearby landlords tended to file for fewer evictions," implying that owner-occupancy is a stabilizing force. If corporate ownership and owner occupancy are truly inverse forces, we should see a strong negative correlation between them. Testing this is the first sanity check before any policy recommendation can be made about promoting local ownership.
Question 2

Has corporate ownership grown faster in majority-white or majority-nonwhite neighborhoods — and what does this pattern suggest about who bears the risk of displacement?

Motivation The Homes for All MA report found that "communities of color, especially Black and Latinx people, faced particularly high rates of eviction." The NYT piece similarly notes stark racial disparities in eviction data. If corporate ownership has been growing most rapidly in neighborhoods already home to communities of color, that would suggest a compounding dynamic: financialization concentrates where vulnerability is highest. This question uses the 2004–2024 time range to assess whether Boston fits this national pattern.
Question 3

Did the COVID-19 pandemic accelerate the pace of corporate acquisition — and which neighborhoods saw the sharpest post-pandemic inflection?

Motivation The Homes for All MA report focuses specifically on COVID-era evictions, noting that corporate landlords continued filing even during the moratorium. This raises a follow-on question: did the economic disruption of the pandemic also accelerate corporate entry into the housing market? If rates of corporate acquisition steepened sharply after 2020, that would suggest COVID acted not just as a crisis for tenants, but as an opportunity for institutional investors — consistent with national reporting on pandemic-era real estate consolidation.
§ 02

Phase 1: Data Overview & Quality

The dataset contains 441 records across 21 Boston neighborhoods, covering 2004–2024 (21 years × 21 neighborhoods). The two core time-varying fields are corp_own_rate and own_occ_rate. All demographic variables (population, race/ethnicity, housing units) are static across years — they reflect a single Census snapshot joined to every year row. This is an important limitation: it means we cannot observe how neighborhood demographics changed over the same 20-year window, only how ownership rates changed against a fixed demographic baseline.

⚠ Data Quality Notes
  • Frozen demographics: Census-derived fields (population, race counts, housing units) are identical for every year within a neighborhood. Any cross-year demographic analysis in this dataset reflects the structure of the join, not real change.
  • No missing values were found anywhere in the dataset — all 441 records are complete for all 40 fields.
  • All 21 neighborhoods have exactly 21 records (one per year), confirming balanced panel coverage with no gaps.
  • Derived vacancy rate (vacant_unit / tot_unit) shows plausible variation (4–22%), with South Boston Waterfront and Downtown exhibiting unusually high vacancy in 2024 — likely reflecting new luxury construction with slow absorption rather than disinvestment.

Q1: The Structural Tradeoff Between Corporate & Owner Occupancy

The central premise of housing stability research is that owner-occupants and corporate landlords represent opposite ends of the landlord spectrum. To test whether this tradeoff manifests structurally in Boston's housing market, I examined the cross-neighborhood correlation between corporate ownership rate and owner occupancy rate.

"The negative correlation between corporate ownership and owner occupancy has grown stronger over time — reaching −0.94 in 2023 — suggesting that as corporate landlords expand, they are systematically crowding out owner-occupants."

Having established that corporate and owner-occupant housing are in structural tension, the next question is whether this expansion has been spatially equitable — or whether corporate landlords have concentrated in neighborhoods where residents of color are most present.

§ 05

Q3: Did COVID-19 Accelerate the Corporate Takeover?

The Homes for All MA report focuses on COVID as a moment of crisis for tenants — but the same period may have been an opportunity for institutional investors. To test this, I fit linear trends to corporate ownership rates for each neighborhood before (2004–2019) and after (2020–2024) COVID, and compared the slopes.

Summary: Main Lessons Learned

Across 20 years of data for 21 Boston neighborhoods, three core findings emerge. First, corporate ownership and owner occupancy are in near-perfect structural opposition: the cross-neighborhood correlation between the two has grown from −0.80 in 2004 to −0.94 in 2023, and every percentage point gained by corporate owners corresponds to lost owner-occupants. This confirms the mechanistic premise underlying the Homes for All MA report's policy recommendation to promote local ownership.

Second, the geography of corporate expansion does not simply track racial composition. Counter to a simple "corporate landlords target communities of color" narrative, the neighborhoods with the largest absolute growth in corporate ownership over 2004–2024 — South Boston Waterfront, Fenway, Charlestown — skew whiter. The most non-white neighborhoods (Roxbury, Mattapan, Hyde Park) have seen corporate ownership triple but from lower baselines. East Boston is the most important exception: a majority Latinx neighborhood that has seen rapid corporate acquisition, consistent with its well-documented gentrification pressure.

Third, COVID-19 was a universal accelerant. Without exception, every neighborhood in Boston saw a higher rate of corporate acquisition in the 2020–2024 period than in any comparable window before the pandemic. This acceleration was sharpest in already-gentrifying neighborhoods, but no neighborhood was immune. The implication is that the window for policy intervention — before corporate ownership reaches levels that structurally preclude owner-occupancy — may be narrowing rapidly in neighborhoods currently at the mid-range of corporate ownership, like East Boston, Brighton, and Jamaica Plain.

Key limitations to carry forward: demographic fields are frozen at a single Census snapshot, preventing analysis of how racial composition itself changed alongside ownership structure. The dataset also does not include eviction filings directly — future analysis linking these ownership trends to the eviction data would substantially strengthen the causal story.
