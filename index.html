Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions

Dataset

The dataset I chose tracks two key property ownership metrics (corporate ownership rate and owner occupancy rate) across 21 Boston neighborhoods from 2004 to 2024. The ownership rates are derived from the City of Boston’s annual property assessment records; note: corporate ownership is identified through keyphrase matching on owner names (flagging entities with markers like “LLC,” “LP,” “Realty,” “Trust,” etc.), while owner occupancy is drawn from a direct OWN_OCC field in the assessment data. We should note that the corporate ownership classification introduces some fuzziness, as not all corporate-owned properties may be captured, and some non-corporate entities may occasionally be misidentified. The dataset also includes a static snapshot of 2020 U.S. Census demographic data for each neighborhood (population totals, racial and ethnic breakdowns, housing unit counts, and group quarters populations), joined to every year row. Because the Census fields do not vary across years, they reflect neighborhood demographics at a single point in time instead of tracking change alongside the ownership rates. I’m interested in this dataset in particular because I thought it'd be interesting to see the 20-year longitudinal window of how the structure of property ownership in Boston has shifted and how that shift maps onto neighborhoods with different racial and economic compositions, as someone who has lived in Boston for most of my life.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. I want to know if there is a structural tradeoff between corporate ownership and owner occupancy and how strong it is across Boston’s neighborhoods.
    Motivation: The Homes for All MA report argues that live-in landlords file fewer evictions, which seems to imply that owner-occupancy is a stabilizing force. If corporate ownership and owner occupancy are really inverse forces, we should see a strong negative correlation between them across neighborhoods. So I want to ask this as a foundational check before drawing conclusions for policy recommendation about promoting local ownership.
  2. Has corporate ownership grown faster in majority-white or majority-nonwhite neighborhoods? What does that suggest about who bears the risk of displacement?
    Motivation: The Homes for All MA report found that communities of color, specifically Black and Latino residents, faced disproportionately high eviction rates. If corporate ownership has been growing fastest in neighborhoods with more people of color living there, then that would point to a compounding dynamic between financialization and racial displacement. I want to use the 2004–2024 window to test whether Boston fits this national pattern.
  3. Did the COVID-19 pandemic accelerate the pace of corporate acquisition and which neighborhoods saw the sharpest post-pandemic inflection?
    Motivation: The Homes for All MA report documents that corporate landlords continued filing evictions even during the moratorium. A related question is whether the pandemic also accelerated corporate entry into the housing market, which is consistent with national reporting on institutional investors exploiting pandemic-era disruption.

Discoveries & Insights

Data Overview & Quality

The dataset contains 441 records across 21 Boston neighborhoods, covering 2004–2024 (21 years × 21 neighborhoods). The two time-varying fields that are central to my analysis are corp_own_rate and own_occ_rate. All demographic variables are static across years. This is because they reflect a single Census snapshot joined to every year row. This means we unfortunately can't track how neighborhood demographics changed over the same 20-year window, only how ownership rates changed against a fixed demographic baseline. In terms of data quality, these were the points:

  • Census-derived fields are identical for every year within a neighborhood. Any demographic analysis here reflects the structure of the join, not real change over time.
  • No missing values were found in any of the 441 records across all 40 fields.
  • All 21 neighborhoods in Boston have exactly 21 records, one per year, so there's no gaps.
  • I calculated vacancy rates using vacant_unit / tot_unit. Those values appear plausible (4–22%), with South Boston Waterfront and Downtown showing higher vacancy in 2024. I think this may reflect new luxury construction with slow absorption.

Question 1: The Structural Tradeoff Between Corporate & Owner Occupancy

From what I've read, the central premise of housing stability research is that corporate landlords and owner-occupants represent opposite ends of the landlord spectrum. Here, I tested some ways to show whether this tradeoff appears structurally across Boston’s neighborhoods.

Summary

So I think our analysis reveals three core things. First, corporate ownership and owner occupancy are in near-perfect structural opposition, where the cross-neighborhood correlation has remained consistently strong from −0.80 in 2004 to −0.88 in 2024, confirming the mechanistic premise behind policies that promote local ownership as a tenant-stability strategy.

Second, the geography of corporate expansion doesn't simply track racial composition. The neighborhoods with the largest absolute corporate growth over 2004 to 2024 (South Boston Waterfront, Fenway, Charlestown) skew whiter and are in active gentrification. The most non-white neighborhoods (Roxbury, Mattapan, Hyde Park) have seen corporate ownership roughly triple but from lower baselines, suggesting comparatively lower displacement risk from this mechanism at this moment in time. East Boston is the most important exception, with a majority-Latino neighborhood with rapid corporate acquisition, making it a high-displacement-risk case to watch.

Third, COVID-19 was pretty much a near-universal accelerant. Nearly all neighborhoods saw a higher rate of corporate acquisition in 2020 to 2024 than before, with Roxbury as the one exception (its post-COVID growth rate of 0.80 pp/year barely changed from its pre-COVID rate of 0.83 pp/year). The window for policy intervention, before corporate ownership reaches levels that structurally foreclose owner-occupancy, appears to be narrowing rapidly, especially in mid-range neighborhoods like East Boston, Brighton, and Jamaica Plain.

Key limitation: As we discussed throught, demographic fields were frozen in this dataset at a single Census snapshot, so we can't observe how racial composition changed alongside ownership structure. Future work linking these trends to eviction filing data would definitely substantially strengthen the causal story.
